## 1. The Landscape of Official Reports

### What Is an "Official Report"?

An official report is a **structured, purposeful document** produced by a government institution or statistical body to communicate factual information, findings, or decisions to a defined audience. Unlike personal writing or academic essays, official reports follow recognized conventions — they must be **accurate, objective, neutral in tone, and traceable** (meaning claims are backed by data or cited sources).

For NSO Nepal, reports are the primary vehicle through which national statistics are shared with government ministries, policymakers, the media, the public, and international partners like the World Bank or UN agencies.

### The Main Types of Official and Statistical Reports

To communicate effectively, NSO staff must navigate different publications. The following table details the core landscape of report types:

| No.   | Report Type                           | Core Purpose                                                       | Primary Audience                  |
| ----- | ------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------- |
| **1** | **Statistical Bulletin / Release**    | Regularly publish key indicators (e.g., CPI, GDP)                  | Government, media, public         |
| **2** | **Press Release**                     | Announce a finding or publication quickly and accessibly           | Media, general public             |
| **3** | **Statistical Abstract / Compendium** | Comprehensive collection of data across sectors                    | Researchers, planners             |
| **4** | **Analytical / Thematic Report**      | In-depth analysis of a specific topic (e.g., poverty, gender)      | Policymakers, donors, researchers |
| **5** | **Census / Survey Report**            | Document methodology, findings, and tables from a census or survey | All stakeholders                  |
| **6** | **Administrative / Internal Report**  | Progress updates, evaluations, meeting minutes for internal use    | NSO management, ministries        |
| **7** | **Technical / Methodology Report**    | Explain _how_ data was collected, processed, and validated         | Technical peers, statisticians    |
| **8** | **Policy Brief**                      | Concise, action-oriented summary for decision-makers               | Ministers, senior officials       |

### A Closer Look at Key Types

#### Statistical Bulletin

This is the workhorse of a statistics office. Published on a fixed schedule (monthly, quarterly, annually), it presents data with minimal narrative — numbers, tables, and brief commentary.

_Example:_ _Nepal Consumer Price Index — Monthly Bulletin_.

#### Press Release

A press release is a **short, structured announcement** (usually 1–2 pages) that highlights the most newsworthy findings from a just-published report or dataset. It is written for **journalists and the public** — not statisticians. It must be simple, direct, and free of jargon.

For NSO Nepal, a press release might announce: _“Nepal’s inflation rose to 6.2% in Baisakh 2082 — NSO Nepal”_.

It is often the **first public-facing document** released alongside a statistical bulletin, and how it is written determines how statistics get reported in the media.

#### Analytical / Thematic Report

Goes beyond numbers to explain _what they mean_. It includes context, comparisons, and sometimes recommendations.

_Example:_ _Nepal Multiple Indicator Cluster Survey (MICS) — Key Findings Report_.

#### Census / Survey Report

The most comprehensive type — covers background, design, methodology, data quality, findings, and annexes. These are major publications that take months to prepare.

_Example:_ _National Population and Housing Census 2078 — National Report_.

#### Policy Brief

Very short (2–4 pages), highly focused, and explicitly recommends action. Written so a Minister can read it in 5 minutes and know what to do.

### Why Does the Distinction Matter?

Each report type has **different rules** for length, tone, structure, and audience. Mixing these up is a common mistake — for example, writing a press release that reads like a methodology report, or publishing a policy brief that buries the recommendation on page 10. Learning which type to use _before you start writing_ saves time and produces far better results.

> 📌 **Key Takeaway** > Official reports are not all the same — they differ by purpose, audience, and structure. The first decision in any writing task is: _What kind of report is this, and who is it for?_

## 2. Choosing the Right Report for the Right Situation

### The Core Question

Before writing a single word, ask yourself **three questions**:

1. **Who is my audience?** (Journalists? Ministers? Statisticians? The general public?)
    
2. **What action or understanding do I want from them?** (To report the news? To make a policy decision? To replicate our methodology?)
    
3. **What is the urgency and occasion?** (Is this a scheduled release? A one-time survey result? An emergency announcement?)
    

Your answers will point directly to the right report type.

### Decision Guide: When to Write What

|Situation|Best Report Type|
|---|---|
|Monthly/quarterly data is ready (CPI, trade, employment)|**Statistical Bulletin**|
|You’re publishing a bulletin and want media coverage|**Press Release** (issued _alongside_ the bulletin)|
|A minister needs a 5-minute read before a meeting|**Policy Brief**|
|You’ve completed a national census or survey|**Census / Survey Report**|
|You want to analyse trends across multiple indicators over time|**Analytical / Thematic Report**|
|A peer institution asks how you collected your data|**Technical / Methodology Report**|
|You need to brief your director on project progress|**Administrative / Internal Report**|
|An international donor wants a country data summary|**Statistical Abstract / Compendium**|

### The Press Release — When and Why to Issue One

A press release should be issued **every time** NSO Nepal publishes data that has public significance — inflation figures, population estimates, employment rates, poverty statistics. It should:

- Come out **simultaneously** with the main statistical release (not days later).
    
- Be **sent directly** to journalists, news portals, and social media teams.
    
- Contain **one or two headline numbers** — not the full dataset.
    
- Be written so a journalist can reproduce the story almost word for word.
    

> ⚠️ **Common Mistake** > Issuing a press release days _after_ the bulletin, when journalists have already sourced data elsewhere (often incorrectly). **Timing is everything.**

### A Practical Example from NSO Nepal

Suppose NSO Nepal has just completed the **Nepal Labour Force Survey 2081**. Here is what you would produce — and in what order:

1. **Technical/Methodology Report** — for statisticians and peer reviewers (internal first)
    
2. **Survey Report** — full findings, published as the main document
    
3. **Analytical Report** — thematic deep-dive (e.g., youth unemployment)
    
4. **Press Release** — issued on launch day, announcing headline findings
    
5. **Policy Brief** — prepared for the Ministry of Labour summarising key policy implications
    

All five are needed. All five are different documents.

> 📌 **Key Takeaway** > The report type is determined by _who reads it_ and _what they need to do with it_. Always identify your audience and purpose before you structure anything.

## 3. Structuring the Outline

### Why Outlining Comes First

Writing without an outline is like building a house without a blueprint. For official reports especially, where accuracy, completeness, and logical flow are non-negotiable, a solid outline:

- Ensures you don't miss critical sections.
    
- Helps multiple authors divide work clearly.
    
- Prevents the report from growing in unplanned directions.
    
- Makes peer review and editing much faster.
    

### The Universal Outline Framework

> ### 📋 Universal Structure Blueprint
> 
> 1. **Front Matter**
>     
>     - Title page
>         
>     - Foreword / Preface
>         
>     - Table of Contents
>         
>     - List of Tables and Figures
>         
>     - Abbreviations
>         
> 2. **Introductory Section**
>     
>     - Background / Context
>         
>     - Objectives
>         
>     - Scope and Coverage
>         
> 3. **Core Content**
>     
>     - Methodology (how data was collected/processed)
>         
>     - Findings / Results (the main substance)
>         
>     - Analysis / Interpretation
>         
> 4. **Concluding Section**
>     
>     - Summary / Conclusions
>         
>     - Recommendations (where applicable)
>         
> 5. **Back Matter**
>     
>     - References / Bibliography
>         
>     - Annexes / Appendices
>         
>     - Glossary
>         

This framework **scales up or down** depending on the report type. A press release uses only items from sections 2 and 3 — compressed into 3–5 short paragraphs. A census report uses everything.

### Tailoring the Outline by Report Type

#### Statistical Bulletin Outline

> Cover $\rightarrow$ Key Highlights $\rightarrow$ Data Tables $\rightarrow$ Short Analytical Notes $\rightarrow$ Methodology Note $\rightarrow$ Contact

#### Press Release Outline

> Headline $\rightarrow$ Dateline $\rightarrow$ Opening paragraph (the news) $\rightarrow$ Key findings (2–3 bullets or short paragraphs) $\rightarrow$ Context sentence $\rightarrow$ Quote from Director General $\rightarrow$ Boilerplate (about NSO Nepal) $\rightarrow$ Contact details

#### Analytical Report Outline

> Cover $\rightarrow$ Foreword $\rightarrow$ Executive Summary $\rightarrow$ Introduction $\rightarrow$ Data Sources & Methods $\rightarrow$ Thematic Findings (by chapter) $\rightarrow$ Conclusions & Recommendations $\rightarrow$ References $\rightarrow$ Annexes

#### Policy Brief Outline

> Title $\rightarrow$ Key Message (1 sentence) $\rightarrow$ Background (5–6 lines) $\rightarrow$ Evidence (2–3 findings) $\rightarrow$ Policy Options $\rightarrow$ Recommended Action $\rightarrow$ Further Reading

### How to Build Your Outline in Practice

1. **Start with purpose**: Write one sentence — _“This report will help [audience] understand/decide [X].”_
    
2. **List the questions your reader will have** — your sections should answer each one.
    
3. **Group related questions** into logical chapters or sections.
    
4. **Order them** from general to specific, or from context to findings to recommendations.
    
5. **Assign approximate length** to each section so you know where the weight of the report falls.
    

> 💡 **NSO Nepal Tip** > For census and survey reports, outlines are often guided by templates from UNFPA, UNICEF, or the UN Statistics Division. Always check whether a template already exists before building from scratch.

> 📌 **Key Takeaway** > An outline is not a table of contents — it is a _thinking tool_. It forces you to decide what the report needs to contain _before_ you write. Invest time here and the actual writing becomes much easier.

## 4. What to Include in Each Section

### The Anatomy of an Official Report

Let's go section by section, covering what each must contain to be effective.

#### Title Page

Must include: report title, subtitle (if any), publishing institution (NSO Nepal), date/period covered, and edition number (for recurring reports). The GoN emblem is standard for NSO Nepal publications.

#### Foreword / Preface

Written by the Director General or a senior official. Typically 1–2 paragraphs. It:

- Contextualises the importance of the report.
    
- Thanks contributors/funders.
    
- Does **not** repeat findings — that is the Executive Summary's job.
    

#### Executive Summary

The most-read section of any long report. It should:

- Stand alone — a reader who reads _only this_ should understand the report's main message.
    
- Be written **last** (after all other sections are finalised).
    
- Be short: 1–2 pages for a major report; 3–5 lines for a bulletin.
    
- Highlight: purpose, key findings, and (if applicable) key recommendations.
    

#### Introduction / Background

Sets the scene. Answers:

- Why was this report written?
    
- What statistical question or policy need does it address?
    
- What is the time period, geography, and population covered?
    
- How does it relate to previous reports in the series?
    

#### Methodology / Data Sources

Critical for any statistical report — this is what makes your findings credible and replicable. Include:

- Data source(s) (administrative records, survey, census, satellite data).
    
- Sample design (if a survey): sampling frame, sample size, stratification.
    
- Data collection period and methods.
    
- Processing steps: editing, imputation, weighting.
    
- Key limitations and caveats.
    

> 📝 For a **press release**, methodology is reduced to one line:
> 
> _“Data is sourced from the Nepal Consumer Price Index survey conducted across 5 ecological zones covering 77 districts.”_

#### Findings / Results

The heart of the report. Structure this by:

- **Thematic chapters** for large reports (e.g., Chapter 3: Employment; Chapter 4: Wages).
    
- **Indicator sections** for bulletins (e.g., 3.1 Overall CPI; 3.2 Food Inflation).
    

Each finding should include:

- The number/fact itself.
    
- A comparison (to previous period, national target, or international benchmark).
    
- A brief interpretation — what does this change _mean_?
    
- A supporting table or chart.
    

#### Analysis / Discussion

Goes beyond presenting data to explaining it:

- What patterns emerge?
    
- What are the possible explanations?
    
- Are there regional, gender, or income-level differences worth highlighting?
    
- How does this compare to other countries or previous surveys?
    

Keep this section **objective** — analysis in an official statistical report is descriptive and comparative, not opinionated.

#### Conclusions and Recommendations

- **Conclusions**: Summarise what the data _shows_.
    
- **Recommendations**: Suggest what should be _done_ (include only if the report brief calls for it — not all statistical reports make recommendations; policy briefs always do).
    

#### Annexes

House technical material that would slow down the main text:

- Full data tables.
    
- Survey questionnaires.
    
- Sampling frame details.
    
- Definitions and classifications used (e.g., NSCO, NACE codes).
    

### The Press Release — Section by Section

Below is the standard, approved block structure for NSO Nepal press statements.

```
                  [NSO NEPAL LOGO / LETTERHEAD]

FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE                        Date: Baisakh 15, 2082 / 28 April 2025

          HEADLINE: Nepal's Inflation Rises to 6.2% in Baisakh 2082
                 (One sentence, factual, and newsworthy)

KATHMANDU --- [Opening paragraph: the single most important fact, written in plain 
language. Answer: what happened, when, how much?]

[Body paragraph 1: Second most important finding. Include a comparison to a previous 
period or benchmark.]

[Body paragraph 2: Context --- what is driving the change?]

[Quote: “...” said [Name], Director General, NSO Nepal.]

[Boilerplate: One standard paragraph about NSO Nepal --- who we are, what we publish, 
website URL.]

For more information contact:
[Name, Title, Email, Phone]

                                    ###
                (Three hash marks signal the end of a press release)
```

> 🌟 **Golden Rule for Press Releases** > The most important information goes **first**. Journalists cut from the bottom — so if your release is trimmed, the headline and first paragraph must still tell the full story.

> 📌 **Key Takeaway** > Every section of a report has a job. When you know that job, you know what to include — and equally importantly, what _not_ to include. Misplaced content erodes a report's credibility.

## 5. Writing with Statistical Precision

### The Language of Official Statistics

Statistical reports must balance two sometimes competing demands: **technical accuracy** and **accessibility**. At NSO Nepal, your reports are read by both statisticians and ministers who may have no statistical background. The goal is precision that is still readable.

### Key Principles

#### 1. Let the number lead, then explain it

- **Weak:** _“There has been a significant upward trend in price levels.”_
    
- **Strong:** _“Consumer prices rose by 6.2% year-on-year in Baisakh 2082, up from 5.8% in Chaitra 2081.”_
    

Always give the actual number first. Avoid vague adjectives like “significant,” “high,” or “considerable” — they mean different things to different readers.

#### 2. Be precise about what you are measuring

Specify:

- The **indicator**: Is it the unemployment _rate_ or the number of _unemployed persons_?
    
- The **reference period**: Month, quarter, fiscal year (always specify whether Nepali or English calendar).
    
- The **population**: All persons aged 15+? Urban households only?
    

#### 3. Distinguish clearly between percentage and percentage points

This is one of the most common errors in statistical writing.

- If inflation rises from **5% to 6%**, it has risen by **1 percentage point** — not “by 1%.”
    
- A 1% _increase_ in a 5% rate would bring it to **5.05%**.
    

This distinction matters enormously in public communication — getting it wrong may cause journalists to misreport your figure by a factor of five.

#### 4. Use tables and figures correctly

Every table and figure must have:

- A **number** (Table 3.1, Figure 2).
    
- A **title** that fully describes what it shows (no need to re-read the text).
    
- A **unit of measurement** in the header or subtitle.
    
- A **source line** at the bottom _(Source: NSO Nepal, NLFS 2081)_.
    
- A **note** for any footnotes, exclusions, or rounding.
    

Keep tables **in the text near where they are referenced** — don't hide them all in annexes.

#### 5. Qualify uncertainty honestly

If estimates carry margins of error, say so. If data from a district is unreliable due to small sample size, flag it. Transparency about data quality is a mark of a credible statistical office, not a weakness.

### Writing the Press Release with Statistical Precision

The press release is the _public face_ of your statistics. Rules here:

- **Round numbers intelligently**: Say _“about 1.2 million persons”_ rather than _“1,213,447 persons”_ in a headline — but give the precise figure in the body or in a linked table.
    
- **Avoid relative comparisons without anchors**: Don't say _“poverty fell by 30%.”_ Say _“poverty fell from 25.2% in 2010/11 to 17.4% in 2022/23.”_
    
- **Define any term a non-statistician might not know**: If you use “labour force participation rate,” briefly define it in brackets the first time.
    

> 📌 **Key Takeaway** > Precision is not about using complex language — it is about using _exact_ language. Numbers must be anchored in time, population, and units. Vague statistical writing erodes public trust in official data.

## 6. Review, Quality Assurance, and Finalization

### Why Quality Assurance (QA) Is Non-Negotiable

A statistical report published with an error — a wrong number, a mislabelled table, an inconsistency between the text and a chart — damages the credibility of the institution, not just the individual author. For NSO Nepal, which is the custodian of Nepal's official statistics, every report represents the office's reputation.

### The QA Checklist

Every report must pass this checklist before publication:

#### Factual / Data Accuracy

- [ ] Do all numbers in the text match the numbers in the tables?
    
- [ ] Are comparisons to previous periods correct?
    
- [ ] Have totals and sub-totals been cross-checked?
    
- [ ] Are percentage change calculations verified?
    

#### Consistency

- [ ] Is the same number written the same way throughout?
    
- [ ] Are table and figure numbers sequential and correctly referenced in the text?
    
- [ ] Is the time period described consistently (e.g., Baisakh 2082 vs. April/May 2025)?
    

#### Language and Clarity

- [ ] Is the executive summary consistent with the findings section?
    
- [ ] Has jargon been defined on first use?
    
- [ ] Are section headings clear and descriptive?
    

#### Formatting and Style

- [ ] Is the NSO Nepal house style applied (fonts, logos, page numbering, headers/footers)?
    
- [ ] Are tables and figures properly titled and sourced?
    
- [ ] Is the Table of Contents accurate and up to date?
    

#### Press Release — Specific Checks

- [ ] Does the headline accurately reflect the most important number?
    
- [ ] Is the release dated and marked “For Immediate Release”?
    
- [ ] Does it include the contact person's details?
    
- [ ] Has a senior official reviewed the quote attributed to them?
    

### The Review Workflow

A robust review process typically involves at least **three layers**:

```
                  [ Author ]
                      │
                      ▼
        [ Subject-matter Peer Review ]
                      │
                      ▼
       [ Statistical / Technical Review ]
                      │
                      ▼
            [ Management Sign-off ]
                      │
                      ▼
                [ PUBLICATION ]
```

For major publications (census, survey reports), NSO Nepal may also conduct:

- **External expert review** (international consultants, academic peers).
    
- **Pre-publication stakeholder consultation** (with relevant ministries).
    

### Finalization Checklist Before Publishing

- [ ] All data verified against source datasets.
    
- [ ] Final text approved by Director General (or delegated authority).
    
- [ ] Press release prepared and ready to send simultaneously.
    
- [ ] PDF locked and file named correctly _(e.g., NSO_CPI_Baisakh2082_Final.pdf)_.
    
- [ ] Report uploaded to NSO Nepal website with correct metadata.
    
- [ ] Press release distributed to media list.
    
- [ ] Social media posts scheduled (if applicable).
    

> 📌 **Key Takeaway** > Quality assurance is a process, not a single proofread. Build review stages into your production schedule — not as an afterthought at the end, but as planned milestones.

## 7. Synthesis: From Blank Page to Published Report

### Bringing It All Together

All the building blocks are now in place. Here is how they connect in a real NSO Nepal scenario.

### Worked Example: NSO Nepal Publishes the _Nepal Consumer Price Index — Baisakh 2082_

**Situation**: It is the end of Baisakh. The monthly CPI data processing is complete. Your job is to manage the publication from start to finish.

#### Stage 1 — Identify what you need to produce

|Document|Type|Audience|
|---|---|---|
|**CPI Monthly Bulletin**|Statistical Bulletin|Government ministries, researchers, public|
|**Press Release**|Press Release|Media, general public|
|**Internal briefing note**|Administrative Report|Director General / NPC|

#### Stage 2 — Build the outline before writing

- **CPI Bulletin Outline:**
    
    > Cover $\rightarrow$ Key Highlights Box $\rightarrow$ 1. Introduction $\rightarrow$ 2. Overall CPI $\rightarrow$ 3. Food Inflation $\rightarrow$ 4. Non-Food Inflation $\rightarrow$ 5. Regional Breakdown $\rightarrow$ 6. Methodology Note $\rightarrow$ Data Tables $\rightarrow$ Contact
    
- **Press Release Outline:**
    
    > Headline $\rightarrow$ Opening (overall CPI figure + change) $\rightarrow$ Food inflation finding $\rightarrow$ Regional highlight $\rightarrow$ Context sentence $\rightarrow$ DG Quote $\rightarrow$ Boilerplate $\rightarrow$ Contact
    

#### Stage 3 — Write each document for its audience

- The _bulletin_ uses precise tables, percentage point language, and full methodology.
    
- The _press release_ uses one headline number, plain language, and a human quote.
    
- The _internal note_ uses bullet points and summary recommendations.
    

#### Stage 4 — Review before publishing

Peer reviewer checks all figures. Manager checks the press release quote. Director General signs off. Everything is scheduled to go out at 10:00 AM on publication day — bulletin, press release, and website update simultaneously.

This sequential process remains uniform for all document workflows:

Every official report — from a 2-page press release to a 300-page census — follows this same logic. The scale changes; the thinking does not.
